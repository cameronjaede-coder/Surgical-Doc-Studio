# When Photons Press Down

## I.

I dream in shapes, in the curve of interface—  
how a button might breathe beneath a finger,  
how intelligence could wear a gentler face.  
The workshop of my mind hums with desire:  
to build the bridge between machine and meaning,  
to craft the questions algorithms learn to ask,  
to shape the way we speak with silicon dreaming.

But then—the other numbers. The true cost.

## II.

Each model trained is a forest of computation:  
servers stacked in rows like redwoods made of steel,  
their roots not soil but cables, their exhalation  
not oxygen but heat that rises, real  
and relentless. Somewhere, a cooling tower  
releases steam into the morning air.  
Somewhere, a river warms. Each training hour  
burns bright with watts I'll never see from here.

## III.

They say a single model's carbon cost  
can equal several cars across their lifetime—  
the churning, learning, iterating, loss  
functions optimizing toward their prime  
while glaciers calve and tundra starts to thaw.  
I wonder: is my contribution complicit?  
Does every design decision that I draw  
rest on foundations more extractive than explicit?

## IV.

And yet—and yet—the possibility persists:  
that AI might help us model climate systems,  
optimize the grid, that in its neural mists  
we'll find the patterns that our wisdom missed.  
That thoughtful design could make these tools more lean,  
more purposeful, more careful with their power.  
That not to build is not to keep things clean,  
but to abandon influence in a crucial hour.

## V.

So here I stand, a designer at the threshold,  
one foot in wonder, one in doubt's cold room.  
The servers hum their binary manifold.  
The planet warms. The future wants a loom  
on which to weave itself—but from what thread?  
Can I pursue this craft with conscience clear,  
or am I adding to the gathering dread,  
one elegant interface at a time, each year?

## VI.

Perhaps the wrestling *is* the work itself—  
to never look away from what it costs,  
to keep the energy reports on my desk's shelf  
beside the user research, gains and loss.  
To advocate for smaller, sharper models,  
for training runs that justify their burn,  
for designs that make these systems less than godlike:  
useful, bounded, questioned at each turn.

## VII.

Perhaps the question isn't whether *to* create,  
but *how*—with what humility and care,  
with what attention to the true weight  
of every computational layer.  
To design with the planet in the room,  
to make the environmental cost visible,  
to choose—again, again—the harder path of zoom:  
more focused tools, more purposeful, more sensible.

## VIII.

I cannot solve this by abstaining, nor  
by building recklessly toward some bright mirage.  
The tension is the territory. The war  
between my passion and my conscience, large  
and constant, is perhaps the only honest place  
from which to work. Not resolution, but  
the daily reckoning, the willingness to face  
what Burns with every gradient descent's cut.

## IX.

So I will design—but not in innocence.  
I'll carry both the wonder and the cost,  
let neither dominate my inner sense  
of what this moment asks, what might be lost,  
what might be saved by thoughtful intervention.  
I'll make my models smaller where I can.  
I'll question every feature's true intention.  
I'll measure what I make by more than what it ran.

## X.

The servers hum. The planet warms. I code.  
Somewhere between denial and despair,  
I'll try to walk a more considered road:  
eyes open to the burden in the air,  
hands moving still to shape what might emerge—  
not savior, not destroyer, but a person  
who chose to wrestle with the urgent urge  
to build, while knowing building makes things worsen.

## XI.

Unless.

Unless the wrestling changes how we build.  
Unless the guilt becomes a guide, not paralysis.  
Unless the carbon budget is fulfilled  
by renewable energy, by analysis  
that makes efficiency the core concern.  
Unless designers like me make the choice  
to measure impact, question, and unlearn  
the myth that progress is a single voice.

## XII.

I dream in vectors still. But now I dream  
in carbon too, in water, heat, and land.  
My interfaces must serve a broader scheme  
than elegance—they must help us understand  
and navigate with less, with clearer sight,  
with tools that justify their hungry glow.  
I'll pursue this work, but not in pure delight—  
in tension, always, with what I know.

---

*For every designer who has felt the pull  
between creation and its consequence,  
between the empty page and the too-full  
planet: may we work with both immense  
ambition and restraint, with joy and grief,  
and may our wrestling make our work more true—  
not comfortable in easy belief,  
but awake to what our choices do.*
