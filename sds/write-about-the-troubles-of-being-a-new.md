## The New Designer's Prototyping Paradox

Landing that first design role often hinges on a cruel irony: you need professional experience to get the job, but you need the job to gain professional experience. Nowhere is this more apparent than in AI-assisted prototyping and design systems work.

Employers want to see polished, interactive prototypes that demonstrate user flows, micro-interactions, and edge cases. They want Figma files that showcase component libraries, design systems, and responsive breakpoints. But as a new designer, you're still learning these tools while simultaneously trying to prove you've mastered them.

The stress intensifies during interview assignments. You're given 48 hours to prototype a feature that professional teams would spend weeks refining. You agonize over every transition, second-guessing whether your navigation makes sense or if your prototype is too simple—or worse, too complicated.

Then there's the technical gap. Senior designers reference prototyping best practices you've never heard of. Your portfolio pieces, created in isolation, suddenly feel amateur compared to the collaborative, real-world projects you're competing against.

The reality? This is where every designer begins. That flawed first attempt isn't failure—it's your foundation. Every revision, every piece of feedback, every rejection shapes your craft. The difficulty isn't evidence of inadequacy—it's the process of transformation itself.
